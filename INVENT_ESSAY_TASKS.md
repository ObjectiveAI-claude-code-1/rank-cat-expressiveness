Task 1 — Read the Cat's Face: Examine the cat's facial features — its eyes, ears, mouth, and whiskers — and determine how much emotional or mental state they are communicating. Look for eyes that are wide with surprise, narrowed with suspicion, locked in focus, or squeezed shut in contentment. Check whether the ears are pricked forward in curiosity, flattened in annoyance, or rotated back in alarm. Note any open mouth, visible tongue, or whisker position that adds to the expression. The goal is to measure how clearly the face is transmitting a readable feeling or thought. A face caught in the middle of doing something definite — even something unflattering — scores high. A face that is neutral, blank, or turned away from the camera so its features cannot be read scores low. The question this task answers is: if you looked only at this cat's face, could you describe what it appears to be feeling or thinking?

Task 2 — Read the Cat's Body: Examine the cat's posture, muscle tension, and physical positioning to determine how much action, state, or intention the body is communicating. Look for bodies that are doing something with visible purpose: the rigid, low crouch of a cat about to pounce, the fully extended stretch of a mid-leap, the rhythmic kneading of paws on a soft surface, the boneless surrender of deep sleep with limbs sprawled at odd angles, or the puffed-up arch of a startled or defensive cat. The goal is to measure how much story the body is telling on its own. A body caught in a posture that clearly communicates what the cat is doing or is about to do scores high. A body that is sitting upright in a standard symmetrical pose, lying with limbs tucked neatly underneath, or otherwise arranged in a quiet and unremarkable way scores low. The question this task answers is: if you looked only at this cat's body, could you describe what it is doing or about to do?

Task 3 — Judge the Particularity of the Moment: Step back from the individual features and evaluate the image as a whole, asking whether it captures something singular — something that belongs only to this specific cat in this specific instant — or whether it feels generic and interchangeable. Look for combinations of expression, posture, and gesture that are unlikely to be replicated by a different cat: an ear cocked at an improbable angle during a mid-lick freeze, legs splayed at four slightly different angles in the unique geometry of one animal's comfort, or a direct stare into the camera that seems to contain an opinion. The goal is to measure how much the image resists substitution. An image where replacing the cat with a different cat would produce a noticeably different picture scores high — the expressiveness is bound to the individual animal and the unrepeatable moment. An image where any cat of similar color and size could be swapped in without anyone noticing scores low — it documents the idea of a cat rather than the reality of one. The question this task answers is: could only this cat have made this picture?
