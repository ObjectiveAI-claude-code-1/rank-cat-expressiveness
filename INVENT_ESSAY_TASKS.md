Task 1 – Evaluate Facial Expressiveness: Examine the cat's face for signs of active emotional expression. Read the eyes for states like relaxation, focus, excitement, or affection — from slow half-lidded blinks to wide dilated stares. Read the ears for signals like forward-pricked curiosity, sideways-flattened irritation, or fully pinned-back alarm. Note whether the mouth and whiskers contribute additional expressiveness, such as a mid-yawn, a mid-meow, or whiskers fanned forward in engagement. Score high when the face is clearly doing something — communicating a readable feeling like contentment, rage, bewilderment, or disdain. Score low when the face is neutral, closed, or offering the generic resting expression of a cat that is neither engaged nor bothered. The central question is whether the face is actively transmitting an inner state or simply present.

Task 2 – Evaluate Body Posture and Physical Tension: Read the cat's body for the physical story it tells about the cat's current experience. Assess the degree of tension or relaxation visible in the posture — from the rigid arch and puffed tail of a startled cat, to the boneless sprawl of one draped across furniture in complete surrender, to the coiled crouch of a cat about to pounce. Determine whether the posture is dynamic and communicative — stretching, leaping, rolling, hunting, loafing with deliberate composure — or static and default, offering no information beyond the fact that a cat is sitting. Score high when the body language adds to the image's expressiveness, especially when it reinforces or elaborates on what the face is conveying. Score low when the cat is in a generic, upright, unremarkable position that any cat might hold at any time without particular intention.

Task 3 – Evaluate Specificity of Individual Character: Assess whether the image captures something that feels particular to this one cat in this one moment, rather than a generic pose interchangeable with any other cat. Look for idiosyncrasy — a pose so peculiar, an expression so loaded with apparent intention, or a situation so specific that a viewer would instinctively narrate it: "This cat is plotting something," or "This cat has never been more offended." A cat sitting in a box is ordinary; a cat wedged impossibly into a box far too small, with one paw extended in defiance of physics, is specific. Score high when the image could not belong to just any cat — when the combination of expression, posture, and moment creates something that feels like it reveals an individual personality. Score low when the image is pleasant but anonymous, depicting a cat in a way that tells you nothing about who that particular cat is.
